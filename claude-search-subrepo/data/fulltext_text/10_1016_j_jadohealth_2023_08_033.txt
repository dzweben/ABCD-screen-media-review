TITLE: Risk Assessment of Maladaptive Behaviors in Adolescents: Nutrition, Screen Time, Prenatal Exposure, Childhood Adversities - Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development Study

ABSTRACT:
Abstract Purpose: We aimed to identify significant contributing factors to the risk of maladaptive behaviors, such as alcohol use disorder or obesity, in children. To achieve this, we utilized the extensive adolescent brain cognitive development data set, which encompasses a wide range of environmental, social, and nutritional factors. Methods: We divided our sample into equal sets (test, validation; n = 3,415 each). On exploratory factor analysis, six factor domains were identified as most significant (fat/sugar intake, screen time, and prenatal alcohol exposure, parental aggressiveness, hyperactivity, family violence, parental education, and family income) and used to stratify the children into low- (n = 975), medium- (n = 967), high-(n = 977) risk groups. Regression models were used to analyze the relationship between identified risk groups, and differences in reward sensitivity, and behavioral problems at 2-year follow-up. Results: The functional magnetic resonance imaging analyses showed reduced activation in several brain regions during reward or loss anticipation in high/medium-risk (vs. low-risk) children on a monetary incentive delay task. High-risk children exhibited heightened middle frontal cortex activity when receiving large rewards. They also displayed increased impulsive and motivated reward-seeking behaviors, along with behavioral problems. These findings replicated in our validation set, and a negative correlation between middle frontal cortexthickness and impulsivity behavior was observed in high-risk children. Discussion: Our findings show altered reward function and increased impulsiveness in high-risk adolescents. This study has implications for early risk identification and the development of prevention strategies for maladaptive behaviors in children, particularly those at high risk. Keywords:  Adolescence, Nutrition, Childhood adversity, Prenatal exposure, Functional MRI

METHODS:
Methods Participants To investigate our study objectives, we used the data from the National Institute on Mental Health (NIMH) Data Archive Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development (ABCD) collection release 4.0 (DOI:  10.15154/1,523,041 ). The study received approval from the institutional review board at each study site, and centralized institutional review board approval was obtained from the University of California, San Diego. All parents provided informed consent, and assent was obtained from the children involved in the study. Of a total 11,878 participants, the sample size was reduced to 6,830 in our analysis based on the amount of available data for total fat and sugar intake. A total of 6,830 children were randomly divided into two sets of equal size: the test set and the validation set, with each set consisting of 3,415 children. The test set was used for developing and testing a composite variable for the risk of maladaptive behavior, while the validation set served to confirm the model’s reliability on an independent sample. Variables for factor analysis We utilized various variables as baseline events in our factor model, including parental education, family income (as indicators of SES), parent/caregiver ethnicity, prenatal exposure to alcohol or caffeine, family history of AUDs, family environment, parents’ behavioral, emotional, and social problems, total fat and sugar intake (collected at 2-year follow-up), screen time usage, and body mass index (BMI)  z-score  (please see  Table 1 ). For more information about each variable used in the factor model and the detailed calculation of the BMI  z-score , please refer to the methods section in Supplemental File S1. These measures were collected at the 2-year follow-up event. Behavioral approach system (BAS) and the behavioral inhibition system (BIS). The BIS/BAS scale is a widely used tool to assess an individual’s approach or avoidance behavior. BAS focuses on appetitive motives and the inclination to move toward desired outcomes [ 25 , 26 ]. A higher BIS score indicates a tendency to avoid potential punishment, while higher scores on BAS subscales reflect positive emotionality (reward responsiveness) and behavioral approach (drive and fun seeking) in relation to potential rewards [ 25 , 26 ]. In our analysis, we utilized BAS drive, fun seeking, reward responsiveness, and BIS sum scores. UPPS-P impulsive behavior scale. In our analysis, we incorporated precalculated baseline scores of positive urgency and negative urgency. Positive urgency relates to the tendency to act impulsively when experiencing a highly positive mood, while negative urgency pertains to impulsive behavior during a significantly negative mood. Both positive and negative urgency have been found to be predictors of an elevated risk for the onset of addictive or other maladaptive behaviors [ 27 , 28 ]. Child behavior. The child behavior checklist is a well-known psychopathological symptom and syndrome assessment tool [ 29 ]. It is a parent-rated questionnaire containing 119 items which are rated on a 3-point Likert scale (0 = not at all true; 1 = somewhat true; and 2 = very true), comprising of eight syndrome scales (anxious/depressed, withdrawn/depressed, somatic complaints, social problems, thought problems, attention problems, aggressive behavior, and rule-breaking behavior), two broad-band scales (internalizing and externalizing problems), and a total problem score, all documented as t-scores. Imaging measures –the ABCD MRI data were collected across 21 research sites using Siemens Prisma, GE 750 and Philips Achieva, and Ingenia 3 T scanners. For our study, we focused on utilizing the 2-year follow-up event data sets of the MID task-based fMRI and structural MRI. Monetary incentive delay (MID) task-based fMRI. The ABCD children performed a modified version of the MID task, where their brain activation was examined during the anticipation and receipt of three conditions: reward ($0.20 or $5), loss (−$0.20 or −$5), or no-incentive ($0). From the 2-year follow-up event data, we focused on the following four primary contrasts: (1) anticipation of reward versus no-incentive, (2) anticipation of large reward versus no-incentive, (3) anticipation of loss versus no-incentive, and (4) reward receipt, positive versus negative feedback. Image preprocessing and regions of interest (ROIs) data calculation were conducted by the Data Analysis, Informatics & Resource Center of the ABCD study, utilizing validated techniques from previous extensive studies [ 30 , 31 ]. Cortical and subcortical regions were parcellated using FreeSurfer v5.3.0, derived from cortical surface reconstruction and subcortical segmentation [ 32 ]. At the individual level, a general linear model implemented in AFNI’s 3dDeconvolve was used to estimate task-related activation, resulting in contrast beta weights [ 33 ]. Average general linear model beta coefficients were computed separately for the two task runs and then averaged across runs for the relevant contrasts. The analysis focused on specific ROIs, including bilateral PFC [middle frontal cortex (MFC) rostral, MFC caudal, ACC rostral, ACC caudal, orbitofrontal cortex (OFC) lateral, and OFC medial], DS (caudate, putamen), ventral striatum (nucleus accumbens), amygdala, insula, posterior cingulate cortex, pallidum, cuneus, precuneus, and thalamus. Our selection of the PFC regions was based on prior literature reporting the role of MFC, ACC, and OFC in reward anticipation [ 22 ]. Structural MRI. The T1 structural magnetic resonance images were processed for gradient nonlinearity distortion correction using scanner-specific, nonlinear transformations. Cortical reconstruction and segmentation were performed using FreeSurfer v5.3.0 by the Data Analysis, Informatics & Resource Center [ 34 ]. To ensure data quality, a combination of automated and manual methods were employed by Data Analysis, Informatics & Resource Center prior to sharing the data through the NIMH Data Archivedatabase. In our current investigation, we utilized the postprocessed structural data, which included cortical thickness, from the same ROIs mentioned earlier for the MID task-based fMRI analysis. The Desikan–Killiany brain registration atlas was utilized for defining the ROIs [ 35 ]. Covariates In regression models, the covariates included were children’s age (in months), sex (male or female), and race/ethnicity (White, Black, Latino/Hispanic, or other), as reported by parents at baseline. Analytic strategy We first conducted an  exploratory factor analysis  (using SAS 9.4 Proc factor procedure) on the test set (n = 3,415 children) using the baseline variables listed in  Table 2 . However, total fat/sugar intake data were only collected at the 2-year follow-up. The baseline variables included parents’ self-behavior t-scores related to various problems (anxious/depressed problems, attention problems, aggressive problems, depression problems, anxiety disorder, attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder problems, antisocial behavior, externalizing, and internalizing problems), prenatal alcohol and caffeine exposure, parental education, ethnicity, alcohol problems, family income, family conflicts (fights in family, family members sometimes get angry and throw things, family members often criticize each other, or family members sometimes hit each other), nutrition (total fat and sugar intake), screen time (weekend and weekday), and BMI  z-scores . Weekend screen time 0.65903 −0.06968 0.15862 0.17383 0.52761 −0.01019 The exploratory factor analysis was conducted using the SAS 9.4 Proc factor procedure with the varimax rotation option. To address multicollinearity, we employed a variable reduction method by selecting the variables that showed the strongest correlation with the within-group composite. Based on this approach, we chose the following variables for the factor analysis: family history of alcohol use, adult self-reported (asr) aggressive measures, and attention deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) measures. Variables with the highest loading within each orthogonal factor in the risk behavior domain (with eigenvalue > 1) were included in the risk score composite. The composite was created by averaging the standardized values of each item, with numeric variables standardized into  z-scores  and binary variables coded as 0 or 1. The risk behavior composite was the mean of these equally weighted individual variables for each participant. If any item was missing for a participant, the composite variable was set as missing for that individual. We also reversed-coded variables when necessary to ensure higher values correlated with greater risk. To check for possible evidence of bias in the study due to selective missingness in the test sample, we created an indicator variable with a value of one if the participant was not included in the risk categorization due to missing data for any of the items that were used to create the composite variable and a 0 for the ones with no missing data. Next, this missing data indicator variable was analyzed to test for statistical associations between the missing data indicator variable and known participant characteristics using the chi-squared test for the categorical variables and the nonparametric Wilcoxon rank-sum test for the continuous variables. Because we examined 23 variables for this sensitivity analysis, a Bonferroni adjusted alpha level of 0.002 was used. To create risk categories, a three-category risk variable was generated by performing a tercile split on the continuous composite variable. Categorizing the composite into groups was chosen because scatterplots depicting the relationship between brain imaging outcomes, and the continuous composite variable did not exhibit a strictly linear pattern with a constant fixed slope throughout the entire range. Instead of exploring complex mathematical models with quadratic and cubic terms, we implemented threshold levels for risk behaviors as a simpler and more practical approach. Although the determination of these thresholds was data-driven, we aimed to validate our findings and assess the reproducibility of our categorization by applying the same rules to an independent data set. This approach balanced practicality and statistical soundness, facilitating meaningful interpretation and application of the findings in a clinical context. We employed  linear mixed effect models  to examine the association between risk groups and behavioral as well as neural changes in children at the 2-year follow-up. We accounted for significant differences in sex and child race/ethnicity across the risk groups and adjusted for these covariates in our analysis. Considering that there are notable site differences in key behavioral and brain measures within the ABCD study, we also included study site as a confounding variable in our analysis. In our models, we treated ABCD study site as a random effect, while risk categories, sex, child race/ethnicity, and age at 2-year follow-up were treated as fixed effects. The covariates used remained consistent across all analyses unless explicitly mentioned. To assess the association between risk groups and parent-rated children’s behavior, we constructed similar regression models. We then conducted three pairwise comparisons (two-sided) to identify differences in behavioral measures (BIS/BAS scores, UPPS negative and positive urgency scores), brain activation patterns, thickness measurements of regions of interest (averaging values from left and right hemispheres), and parent-rated children’s behaviors. To account for multiple pairwise comparisons, we applied a Bonferroni correction with an adjusted alpha level of 0.05/3 = 0.0167 (all Bonferroni  p  values in the table were multiplied by three; thus, any Bonferroni  p  value < .05 is considered significant). Considering the exploratory nature of the outcomes evaluated within the training sample, we exercised caution in reporting only statistically significant findings from the training sample that were also replicated in the test sample. This approach aimed to ensure the reliability and reproducibility of our results. By adopting these methodological considerations, we aimed to provide robust and interpretable findings in our analysis of the association between risk groups, behavioral measures, brain activation patterns, and parent-rated children’s behaviors. We examined the correlation of regional activation pattern and thickness measures with impulsivity behaviors (positive and negative urgency) of high- and medium-risk children using nonparametric Spearman’s correlations. For all descriptives, regression models, and correlation analyses, we used SPSS v.28 (IBM Corp, Armonk, NY) and addressed missing values in linear mixed effect models using the restricted maximum likelihood approach.

Missing data Overall, 14% of the participants were excluded from the test data set used to develop the model due to missing data. There was no statistically significant evidence of a clinical or statistical imbalance in missing data for each of the following covariates: age, sex, race, weight, waist circumference, height, BMI  z-scores , family income, parental education, screen time weekdays and weekends, total fat intake, total sugar intake, and aggressive behavior asr syndrome scale total score, attention problems asr syndrome scale total score, hyperactivity-impulsivity asr Diagnostic Statistical Manual-5-oriented scale total score, prenatal caffeine exposure, prenatal alcohol exposure, parental alcohol problem, we fight a lot in our family, family members sometimes get so angry they throw things, family members often criticize each other, and family members sometimes hit each other (see  Supplemental Table 1 ). There was, however, a statistically significant higher rate of missing data for Black, Asian, and other (>17%) compared to White (11.7%) participants. After the Bonferroni multiple comparison adjustment, the difference in the proportion of missing data between Whites and Black participants remained statistically significant ( p  < .001), as well as, between Whites and mixed/other race participants ( p  = .03). While the rate of missing data was significantly lower for White participants, over 75% of the Asians and Black participants did provide data. Lastly, participants with prenatal alcohol exposure (5.4%) had a significantly (Bonferroni  p  value = .001) lower rate of missing data compared to those without prenatal alcohol exposure (10.1%). While we found no overwhelming evidence of informative missingness, we acknowledge in the limitation section that there may be factors that were not measured that may have had an impact on missing data. Due to the relatively low rate of missing data (14%) and the large number of primary analyses run on the test and validation sample, we did not add additional sensitivity analyses employing multiple imputation or replacement methods.

Correlation of regional activation and thickness measures with impulsivity behavior To explore the connections between brain activity, cortical thickness, and impulsivity, we performed a bivariate correlation analysis. Specifically, we examined the relationship between neural activation patterns and thickness measurements of the insula and MFC, with impulsivity measures (both negative and positive urgency) in low-, medium-, and high-risk children separately using the test sample. Our analysis employed a bivariate Spearman correlation to determine any significant associations between the estimated values (activation during reward vs. no-incentive anticipation) and the thickness values of the insula and MFC with impulsivity behavioral measures. The results yielded interesting findings. In high-risk children, a significant negative correlation was observed between the thickness of the caudal MFC and both negative (r = −1.00;  p  = .004) and positive (r = −0.07;  p  = .04) urgency behaviors. In these children, no significant correlations were identified between the estimated values of the caudal MFC as well as the estimated values and thickness measures of the rostral MFC and insula with impulsivity measures. Notably, no significant brain-behavior correlations were identified in low- and medium-risk children (refer to  Supplemental Figures 1  and  2  for further details).

Group classification Our factor analysis yielded six orthogonal factors: Factor 1: SES (family income, parental education, parental ethnicity); Factor 2: parental behavior (aggressiveness and ADHD problem); Factor 3: nutrition (total fat and sugar intake); Factor 4: family environment (conflict and fight questions); Factor 5: screen time exposure (weekday and weekend); and Factor 6: prenatal exposure (caffeine and alcohol). Parental alcohol problem domain did not show an eigenvalue>1 in the factor model. A composite variable was then created using variables with a rotated factor loading of at least |0.4| as proposed by Hair et al. [ 36 ]. This threshold ensured that only variables with a substantial contribution to the underlying constructs were considered while minimizing the inclusion of redundant variables. The following variables with the highest loading values (>0.4 or <−0.4) on at least 1 of the six factors were added to the composite variable: parental education, aggressiveness and ADHD problem, family income, total fat and sugar intake, family members sometimes hit each other, prenatal alcohol exposure, weekday and weekend screen times ( Table 2 ). Correlation of regional activation and thickness measures with impulsivity behavior To explore the connections between brain activity, cortical thickness, and impulsivity, we performed a bivariate correlation analysis. Specifically, we examined the relationship between neural activation patterns and thickness measurements of the insula and MFC, with impulsivity measures (both negative and positive urgency) in low-, medium-, and high-risk children separately using the test sample. Our analysis employed a bivariate Spearman correlation to determine any significant associations between the estimated values (activation during reward vs. no-incentive anticipation) and the thickness values of the insula and MFC with impulsivity behavioral measures. The results yielded interesting findings. In high-risk children, a significant negative correlation was observed between the thickness of the caudal MFC and both negative (r = −1.00;  p  = .004) and positive (r = −0.07;  p  = .04) urgency behaviors. In these children, no significant correlations were identified between the estimated values of the caudal MFC as well as the estimated values and thickness measures of the rostral MFC and insula with impulsivity measures. Notably, no significant brain-behavior correlations were identified in low- and medium-risk children (refer to  Supplemental Figures 1  and  2  for further details).



DISCUSSION:
Discussion Using a large sample of diverse measures from the ABCD study, our factor analysis yielded 10 variables (fat and sugar intake, prenatal alcohol exposure, parent aggressiveness and ADHD, parental education, weekend and weekday screen time, physical violence among family members, and family income) that were used to characterize these children into low, medium, and high-risk groups. On further analysis, at 2-year follow-up, elevated BAS fun seeking and drive scores were observed in both high- and medium-risk adolescents relative to their low-risk peers, reflecting their propensity toward affective and behavioral responsivity to rewarding stimuli. Elevated scores on these two BAS scales have been associated with low levels of harm avoidance and problematic alcohol use, including among adolescents [ 37 ]. In a recent study, adolescents who initiated substance use early had higher baseline BAS scores than adolescents who had not initiated substance use early on in life [ 38 ]. Therefore, enhanced motivation for seeking rewards, in general, appears to be a risk factor for the development of maladaptive behaviors. The high-risk (vs. low-risk) children revealed elevation in all parent reported behavioral problem traits (anxious/depressed; withdrawn/depressed; somatic complaints; social/thought/attention/internalizing/externalizing/total problems; rule breaking/aggressive behavior). Some of these problems (attention/externalizing/total problems; aggressive behavior) were also observed in medium-risk (vs. low-risk) children. Interpretation of our findings in light of existing literature suggests that in addition to childhood adversity (e.g., poverty, low parental education, or exposure to violence), prenatal alcohol exposure, consumption of a high-fat/high-sugar diet, and increased screen time may contribute to poor inhibitory control in these high-risk children. Higher screen time exposure in children has been associated with increased consumption of high-sugar and high-fat foods [ 39 ]. Furthermore, parental education is a known determinant of a child’s eating behavior, such that children whose mothers had a higher educational level consumed more healthy diets (fruit and vegetables) than those with less-educated mothers [ 40 ]. Family income status is also associated with the food intake habits of children. Children with high SES tend to follow nutrition guidelines and avoid unhealthy food compared to those with lower SES [ 41 ]. BOLD findings during the MID task revealed lower MFC, thalamus, and insular activation in high-risk (vs. low-risk) children during anticipation of reward/large reward or loss avoidance. However, during reward receipt, elevated frontal and precuneus activations were seen in high-risk children (vs. low-risk). These data fit with models suggesting that risk for addiction includes a phenotype that shows disrupted responses to reward, which in our results was observed for the expectation of reward. Prefrontal cortices are crucial for controlling reward-seeking behavior, value attribution, and decision-making [ 42 ]. Early disruption of prefrontal cortical function, including MFC, may contribute to a constellation of impaired activities like increased disinhibition [ 43 ], aberrant salience assignment [ 42 ], and eventually poor decision-making behavior. Immaturity of the prefrontal executive control systems may account for the dysregulation of reward processing in high-risk adolescents. As greater reward was associated with greater risk in this task, a developmentally immature and less active executive system could push adolescents toward greater risk-taking. This interpretation is consistent with several other studies wherein diminished PFC activation is seen when adolescents anticipate responding for gains. A triadic model of adolescent reward processing suggests that adolescents demonstrate heightened reward sensitivity and an overactive reward system [ 43 ]. Adolescents with a developmental deficit in behavior control may experience risk uncertainty and intermittent reinforcers as rewarding [ 44 ]. The more salient the reward, the more likely a prefrontal dopaminergic response will occur that is sufficient to facilitate the formation of a conditioned association [ 45 ]. Children exposed to adverse conditions and prenatal alcohol exposure tend to have self-regulation difficulties characterized by an imbalance between motivational and behavioral control processes [ 46 , 47 ]. Furthermore, the blunted PFC activation in high-risk children possibly reflects their reduced sensitivity to reward, consistent with the hypothesis that these children are at risk for seeking more intense rewards to compensate for a potential reward deficit. Our findings align with a recent electroencephalography study wherein adolescents with higher environmental exposure to nicotine had reduced P3 amplitudes on reward anticipation during the MID task. These adolescents were less sensitive to monetary reward anticipation and were at high risk for smoking initiation [ 48 ]. We also noted attenuation in MFC activation of high-risk children (vs. low-risk) during loss avoidance, perhaps reflecting lower adaptiveness to decision-making for monetary gains or losses. Lower MFC, insular, and thalamus activation during reward anticipation in high-risk children (vs. low-risk) seen in our study aligns with prior reports on children exposed to high levels of life adversity [ 1 ]. High levels of childhood stress compared to low stress levels might lead to reduced attention to reward signals. The argument can be used to support our findings; however, we did not see a correlation between the neural activation patterns and self-report attentional problems in these children. Our findings can be interpreted to reflect reduced reward sensitivity or attention difficulties, but at this point the self-report surveys do not appear to be sensitive or specific enough to show significant associations with trait behavior. Therefore, we can only speculate on the possible significance of MFC activation differences between risk groups. Of note, self-report measures capture very general behaviors that span multiple domains and are perhaps not as good at measuring specific behaviors as impulsivity, inhibition, or self-regulation tasks. Indeed, self-report and task measures of self-control and impulsivity often do not correlate at all [ 49 , 50 ]. Future studies could employ additional cognitive tasks, which might probe more precise behaviors and potentially reveal closer relationships with brain activity than self-report surveys. In addition, we observed reduced thickness in the insular and frontal regions among both high- and moderate-risk children compared to low-risk children. Our study also uncovered a significant negative correlation between the thickness of the MFC and both negative and positive urgency behaviors, specifically in high-risk children. These findings indicate that the thinning of the MFC could potentially contribute to the heightened impulsivity observed in these children. The vulnerability of high-risk children to elevated impulsiveness and motivation to gain reward could relate to their higher exposures to several adverse factors including fat and sugar intake, prenatal alcohol exposure, parent aggressiveness and ADHD, poor parental education, weekend and weekday screen time, physical violence among family members, and low family income. Preclinical studies have documented the adverse effects of a high-fat/sucrose diet in adolescent rats in whom it resulted in lower expression of perineuronal nets in the infralimbic cortex of the medial PFC [ 51 ]. Adults exposed to poverty early in life exhibited reduced OFC volumes [ 52 ], which could be a possible neural signature reflecting the negative impact of stressors like low family income on impulse control. The present study has many strengths: identifying the most discriminative variables for the development of maladaptive behaviors using a large sample in whom a wide range of childhood adversity problems were assessed, relying on well-established measures and a statistical model to identify complex relationships between risk factors and neural problems of interest, and with a built-in replication analysis strategy to ensure reproducibility. Also, we took advantage of this longitudinal study to build our risk groups using baseline measurements (nutrition data collected only at 2-year follow-up) and then test differences in neural and behavioral indices across these risk groups based on their 2-year follow-up BOLD and behavioral measurements. The findings support our risk categorization; however, no causality can be established at this point. To determine which children ultimately develop a substance use disorder, it is crucial to follow-up with these children until early adulthood in future studies as the ABCD children age. Even though we controlled for known covariates, the observed associations may still be affected by residual confounds. Although we did not apply Bonferroni adjustment across brain regions, we did correct for all pairwise comparisons within each brain region and validated our findings on an independent sample set. The effect size magnitudes for our brain findings were relatively small, which most likely pertains to the significant differences in our risk groups ( p  < .001) in many regards (sex and race/ethnicity). The small effect sizes seen in our and other studies using this large ABCD data set [ 53 ] may be due to limitations of the sample, including overrepresentation of high-functioning adolescents and their families. We could not account for parental race, as the factor model cannot accommodate nominal variables with more than two categories. Further, dichotomizing race into Black and White categories would have significantly reduced our sample size. However, we did address income, education, and ethnicity in our model as other known confounders for race. While the findings of the present study have high public health significance, it is important to cautiously interpret the results for practical clinical significance. The identified risk factors may be potentially malleable and future target for intervention, opening avenues for future research to confirm our findings. Although we have a built-in replication set, it will be ideal to replicate and ensure the consistency of these findings using an independent sample with differing methods and sampling techniques. Further, the findings of exploratory factor analyses can be difficult to replicate across samples. The tercile split approach we used for threshold determination has some limitations, such as its arbitrary nature (e.g., database-linked) and potential loss of statistical power compared to a continuous approach, which may limit the generalizability of the findings. There is a possibility that the outcomes we obtained may not be reproducible in other samples due to differences in data collection methods. In addition, since some of the risk factors in the model may involve changes in the parents’ sensitivity to reward, such as drug and alcohol use and dietary preferences, it is imperative to ascertain the extent to which the increased risk in their children is due to environmental factors as opposed to heritability. To address these limitations and facilitate replication, it may be worthwhile to explore the use of large-scale data sets, such as the Generation R study, which is a prospective longitudinal study that tracks individuals from prenatal stages to early adulthood. This study collects a wide range of measures, similar to those in the ABCD study, including physical, behavioral, cognitive, and MRI data [ 54 ]. Conclusion Overall, these findings are consistent with the premise that a difficult environment, including parental aggressiveness or hyperactivity, and low education or income status, high fat/sugar intake, greater screen time, and prenatal alcohol exposure, are major determinants of adverse behavioral outcomes and elevated risk for addiction in adolescents ( Supplemental Figure 3 ). Further lower reward sensitivity, attentional deficit, elevated impulsivity, and behavioral problems were identified in children at high risk for maladaptive behaviors.
