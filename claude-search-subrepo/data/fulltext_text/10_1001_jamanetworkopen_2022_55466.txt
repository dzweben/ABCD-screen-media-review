TITLE: Association of Physical Activity and Screen Time With Body Mass Index Among US Adolescents

ABSTRACT:
Key Points Question What are the associations and interactions of physical activity and screen time categories with body mass index and overweight and obesity among adolescents? Findings In this cross-sectional study of 5797 US adolescents aged 10 to 14 years, a combination of high screen time (hours per day) and low physical activity (step count per day) was associated with overweight and obesity. Meaning These findings suggest that high step count may not offset overweight and obesity risk for adolescents with high screen time and that low screen time may not offset overweight and obesity risk for adolescents with low step count.

METHODS:
Methods We analyzed cross-sectional data from year 2 (September 10, 2018, to September 29, 2020) of the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development (ABCD) Study (4.0 release, involving participants aged 10-14 years), in which participants wore a wireless digital step-tracking device (Fitbit; Alphabet, Inc). Data were analyzed from July 8 to December 20, 2022. The ABCD Study is the largest long-term longitudinal study of adolescent brain development and health in the US. From 2016 to 2018 (year 0), 11 875 children were recruited from 21 racially and ethnically diverse sites across the country. Descriptions of the study sample, recruitment, procedures, and measures have been previously described. 13  For the current analyses, participants with missing data for step count, screen time, or anthropometric variables were excluded (eMethods in  Supplement 1 ), leaving a total sample of 5797 adolescents. Centralized institutional review board approval was received from the University of California, San Diego. The University of California, San Francisco, approved this secondary data analysis. Study sites obtained approval from their local institutional review boards. Caregivers provided written informed consent, and each adolescent provided written assent. This study followed the Strengthening the Reporting of Observational Studies in Epidemiology ( STROBE ) reporting guideline for cross-sectional studies. Exposure Variables Screen Time Screen time data were collected using the ABCD Study Screen Time Questionnaire, which was completed by each adolescent. 14  The questionnaire was divided by weekdays and weekends, asking participants to self-report how many hours per day were spent engaging with several different types of media, including television shows and movies, videos, video games (single player and multiplayer), texting, social media, and video chat. The time across all types of media was summed to form a composite amount of time spent using screens per weekday and weekend. The total mean of recreational screen use was calculated as the weighted sum (weekday mean multiplied by 5 plus weekend mean multiplied by 2, with the sum then divided by 7). Self-reported screen use has been found to have a significant moderate positive correlation ( r  = 0.49;  P  < .001) with an objective passively sensed smartphone app in the ABCD Study. 15  Another study 16  found that self-reported measures of television viewing were significantly moderately correlated (Spearman  P  = .54;  P  < .001) with an objective electronic television monitor and had a high level of agreement, with 95% of values within 4 hours of the mean. Similar self-reported television-watching measures have had acceptable test-retest reliability (7-day test-retest intraclass correlations of 0.76-0.81). 17 , 18  We categorized total recreational screen time in 4-hour increments given that previous studies have identified 4 hours per day to be a threshold associated with overweight and poor mental health outcomes in adolescent populations, 19 , 20 , 21 , 22  and other nationally representative surveys of adolescent screen time have used similar categories (ie, 4 hours per day and 8 hours per day). 23  Screen time was grouped into 3 categories: 0 hours to 4 hours per day (low; reference category), more than 4 hours to 8 hours per day (medium), and more than 8 hours per day (high). Steps per Day Step counts per day were collected via wearable digital devices (Fitbit Charge series) over a 3-week period (21 days). Previous research 24 , 25  has found that these digital devices offer an accurate and reliable estimation of daily step count as a measure of accumulated physical activity in adolescents over time. We followed best practices to extract, filter, and process data established by the ABCD Study. 24 , 25  Within each adolescent's 3-week study protocol, comprising data collected between November 2018 and November 2020 (year 2), we included all days with more than 599 daily minutes of waking wear time and a minimum of 1000 steps per day, consistent with previous studies. 26 , 27 , 28 , 29  Given that previous studies 1 , 30  have identified 12 000 steps as a threshold for meeting the daily physical activity guideline of 60 minutes of moderate to vigorous intensity activity and that 6000 steps per day is approximately one-half of the physical activity guideline dose, total steps per day were grouped into 3 categories: 1000 steps to 6000 steps per day (low), more than 6000 steps to 12 000 steps per day (medium), and more than 12 000 steps per day (high; reference category). Outcome Variables Weight (measured using the Healthometer 844KL High Capacity Digital Bathroom Scale; Health o meter Professional Scales) and height (measured using a carpenter’s square steel tape measure) were assessed by the interviewer at each site. BMI was computed using the standard formula (weight in kilograms divided by height in meters squared 2 ). BMI was converted into sex- and age-specific percentiles in accordance with Centers for Disease Control and Prevention growth curves and definitions. Individuals were classified as having overweight or obesity if their BMI was in the 85th percentile or higher. 31 Covariates Sex (female or male), race and ethnicity (American Indian, Asian, Black, Hispanic or Latinx, White, or other [no specific racial and ethnic groups were defined in the ABCD data set; write-ins were allowed on the self-report, but these data were not available]), household income, highest parental educational level, and parental marital status were self-reported by adolescents and/or their parents. These variables have previously been reported to be associated with physical activity 32  and screen time 33  as well as BMI. 34  Given that the COVID-19 pandemic occurred during the data collection period (November 2018 to November 2020), we created a COVID-19 variable based on the dates of device data collection (before pandemic, before and during pandemic, and during pandemic), defining March 13, 2020, as the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic in the US. Statistical Analysis Data analysis was performed using Stata software, version 15.1 (StataCorp LLC). Descriptive statistics, including means, SDs, and percentages, were calculated. Linear regression analyses were conducted to estimate associations between exposure variables (recreational screen time and/or step count categories) and BMI percentile (continuous outcome). 35  Modified Poisson regression models transformed to risk ratios (RRs) were conducted to estimate associations between exposure variables (screen time and/or step count categories) and overweight or obesity (binary outcome). 35  Overweight or obesity was relatively common (>10% of participants); therefore, modified Poisson regression models (ie, using robust SEs) were used instead of logistic regression models because odds ratios produced by logistic regression models are likely to be overestimates of the RRs in such instances. 36 For each outcome, we ran 3 models with different exposure combinations: model 1, including screen time only; model 2, including step count only; and model 3, including screen time and step count (joint associations). All 3 models were adjusted for age, sex, race and ethnicity, household income, parental educational level, parental marital status, and data collection period. We examined exposure variables as continuous and categorical variables. We assessed interactions between screen time and step count categories for the association with BMI percentile. Given evidence of significant interactions, we created a 9-category exposure (combinations of 3 screen time categories and 3 step count categories) to estimate the association of each screen-step category combination with BMI percentile. We conducted a sensitivity analysis using only data collected before the COVID-19 pandemic. All analyses incorporated sample weighting to approximate the demographic distribution of the American Community Survey of the US Census. 35  Because each participant was assigned a different propensity weight, only weighted percentages (vs absolute numbers and percentages) of participants were reported. Two-sided  P  < .05 was considered statistically significant.

RESULTS:
Results Among 5797 adolescents included in this analysis, 50.4% were male, 49.6% were female, 37.7% were from racial and/or ethnic minority groups (3.1% American Indian, 5.3% Asian, 12.0% Black or African American, and 17.3% Hispanic or Latinx), and 61.0% were White, with a mean (SD) age of 12.0 (0.6) years. Adolescents reported a mean (SD) screen time of 6.5 (5.4) hours per day, an overall mean (SD) step count of 9246.6 (3111.3) steps per day over the course of 21 days, and 35.0% were classified as having overweight or obesity ( Table 1 ). DISCUSSION:
Discussion In this cross-sectional study of a national demographically diverse sample of adolescents aged 10 to 14 years who participated in the ABCD Study in the US, several research gaps identified in the 2018  Physical Activity Guidelines Advisory Committee Scientific Report 6  have been addressed. We found that high recreational screen time was associated with higher BMI percentile and overweight or obesity, even when accounting for step count as a measure of physical activity. Second, we found a dose-response association between step count categories based on data from a wearable digital device (Fitbit) and the risk of overweight or obesity. Third, we found effect modification between screen time and physical activity for screen time of less than 8 hours per day and step count of more than 6000 steps per day. Conversely, higher physical activity did not attenuate the risk of high BMI among those with high recreational screen time; similarly, lower screen time did not attenuate the risk of high BMI among those with low physical activity. Early adolescence is an important developmental period because obesity in adolescence persists into adulthood. 37 We found that medium or high levels of screen time (compared with low levels) were associated with higher BMI percentile and overweight or obesity. Findings were similar even when accounting for physical activity in the models. A dose-response association was observed, in which high screen time was associated with the highest risk of overweight or obesity, followed by medium and low screen time. This finding is consistent with results from a previous study, 38  which reported positive associations between screen time and obesity in both children and adolescents. Recreational screen time is mostly a sedentary behavior, which can lead to an increase in caloric consumption through mechanisms such as snacking in the absence of hunger and exposure to advertisements that promote unhealthy foods. 39 , 40 , 41  Our study addresses previous limitations in the literature by including several contemporary modalities in the screen time measure, finding a dose-response association between categories of screen time use and BMI percentile, adjusting for step counts, and focusing on early adolescence. We also found that medium or low levels of step count (compared with high levels of step count) were associated with higher BMI percentile and risk of overweight or obesity, even when accounting for screen time. A dose-response association was observed, in which the low step count category was associated with the highest risk of overweight or obesity, followed by the medium and high step count categories. These findings build on previous literature 42  examining associations between self-reported physical activity and overweight or obesity in adolescents by using an objective and clinically relevant step count. We also used objective step measures from wearable digital devices and categories approximately corresponding to physical activity recommendations, which could inform future guidance for step counts for adolescents. When step counts were low or screen use was high, there was no significant interaction between screen time and step count. That is, no amount of steps per day attenuated the association between high recreational screen use and BMI percentile. Similarly, the association between low step count and high BMI was not attenuated by minimal screen use. Overall, our findings are consistent with those of a previous study 43  analyzing the interaction between sedentary behavior and physical activity and its association with BMI in US adults, which found a significant difference in BMI only by time spent engaging in physical activity for adults watching 189 minutes per day or less of television and/or movies. In contrast, adults watching 60 minutes per day or less of television and/or movies had lower mean BMI independent of their amount of physical activity. Taken together, our results are consistent with those reported in previous studies involving adults 43  and adolescents, 12  which suggest an upper limit of screen time hours is needed to observe a compensatory consequence of physical activity for BMI. Our study also adds to the literature by expanding recreational screen time to include contemporary modalities (texting, social media, and video chat), which is why the threshold may be slightly higher than the 189 minutes observed for television and movies, and by using step count as an objective measure of physical activity in adolescents. We suggest that step count and screen time categories for adolescents may be used to inform public health guidelines for adolescents. Strengths and Limitations This study has several strengths. First, the study includes a large and sociodemographically diverse population-based sample. Second, for physical activity measures, the study uses objective data that were collected over 21 days (longer than the typical 7-day protocol), which limits self-reporting bias and decreases SE. Third, for the screen time measures, the study includes several different types of screen mediums used by adolescents rather than just computer and television. This study also has limitations, including its cross-sectional design. The device used to measure step count (Fitbit) is a wearable commercial product but has been reported to have good performance in this age group relative to gold-standard activity monitors. 25  Device data were collected for a 3-week period (21 days), which may not be representative of a participant’s physical activity or the intensity of activity over the course of 1 year. To overcome this limitation and explore potential seasonal disparities, future studies may use longitudinal data from digital step-tracking devices over longer durations. While we included only days with more than 599 minutes of waking wear in the analysis, we did not control for variations in wear time that were higher than this threshold, thus producing a potential limitation with regard to differences in participants’ total wear time and variations in wake vs sleep time. Screen time measures were based on self-reported data, which are subject to recall and reporting bias, have low precision, and do not assess content exposure. Even though self-reported measures may be approximations of actual use, it is likely that they are underreported, which could bias findings toward the null hypothesis. 15 Future studies, including those using the ABCD data set, could take advantage of more intensive approaches that monitor device use in relation to BMI. 44  BMI may be a poor proxy for obesity given that it cannot differentiate fat from lean mass. 45  Excluded participants were more likely to be racial and/or ethnic minority adolescents from lower socioeconomic backgrounds; this group has been previously found to report greater screen time and less physical activity and to have greater BMI percentiles. 3 , 5 , 33 , 46  This potential selection bias could bias findings toward the null hypothesis. Another limitation is the potential for unmeasured confounders, although we controlled for site and sociodemographic factors as well as the COVID-19 pandemic.



CONCLUSION:
Conclusions This cross-sectional study addresses research gaps identified by the  Physical Activity Guidelines Advisory Committee Scientific Report  by providing evidence that specific categories of screen time and step counts were associated with overweight or obesity. In this study, screen time of more than 4 hours per day and physical activity of less than 12 000 steps per day were associated with a higher risk of overweight or obesity among a racially diverse population-based sample of US adolescents. The results suggest that the combination of less than 4 hours per day of screen time and greater than 12 000 steps per day of physical activity is beneficial to minimize the risk of higher BMI percentile. Future research should use a longitudinal study design and analyze additional chronic disease risk factors and outcomes, including sleep, which would further inform physical activity and screen time guidelines for adolescents.
